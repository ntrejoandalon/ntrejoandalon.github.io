Stitch-onary

Project Idea

        The vision of this project was simple: a mobile app to help crocheters and knitters keep track of where they are at in the project and what else they need to do. Throughout, it evolved into a web and mobile application, but it has always maintained true to that. It’s an all to common story where a person comes back to a project, be it from a few weeks or just that morning, and has to take a few minutes to gain their footing. Fiber arts are time-consuming as a fact, but having to count rows to figure out where they’re at doesn’t need to be.  

        In the beginning we had Sam. Sam is a college girl with big dreams and an ever-increasing yarn stash. She’s switching between two projects: a blanket and a plushie. While she’s on the bus she takes her smallest project to keep her busy and she uses our app so she can track on less thing while on the go and feel secure that she has made the pattern correctly. Later, when she opens the app up again, it will be ready right where she left it. In it all, the main benefit the app brings is peace of mind and time. It assures the user that they have done the pattern correctly, with the right number of stitches in each row of the correct colors. It also helps them save the time they would have normally used finding their place in the project or counting their stitches to check if they’re right.

        I decided to pursue this project as it is something I have always struggled with in my hobbies. I have many projects and have needed a way to organize as well as way to minimize the time it takes to acclimate from one to another. I also like to work on the go which sometimes makes it more difficult to keep track of my pattern unless I know it very well. Stitch-onary, the name of our app, is a way to circumvent these issues. Going off of the story, we decided to have both a mobile and web view for our project because although I may take things on the go, it is just as important a problem for people that work at home. Additionally, while the mobile is more travel friendly, web is more useful for long periods of crochet or knitting as it is easier to view and navigate.

This project did not happen individually. I was working with Kyana to make sure to get the best possible product. Although we were working as team, there was a very clear division of who did what on Stitch-onary. All the way from sketches to the final prototype, I worked on the mobile version whil Kyana worked on the desktop version. In doing so, it made it simpler to work as a team as there were ways both of us could progress regardless if we were working concurrently or individually. Later on, as you’ll see, this causes some problems with the two types being unique, and throughout this post we will be focused on the mobile rather than the desktop version.

Sketches

        

        Although I knew we weren’t going to develop out all the pages, I decided to still sketch the application out on mobile to get a good flow from page to page. The initial sketches are down below.

Our main focus when designing this was making it clear what to click and why to click it. Throughout the process, I’ve strived to have larger buttons as well as making the stitch count highly visible. In doing so, I hoped to mitigate the discomfort from changing the focus of vision from further away to a close screen.

All of the mockups and prototypes were made using the design system below. This system has changed as the prototypes further develop, but there are a few features that have remained the same. For example, purple. While we have changed the shade purple, it was always decided to be purple because of it’s relation to creativity. As a project where one of the core values is creativity, we wanted to make sure our project reflects it down to the colors. We also opted out of a pure white for a cream background to add in a softer balance between the colors and make it more friendly on the eyes. For font, we prioritized a serif font to make it easy to read and thus easy to glance at, update, and keep working on.

        Using all of this, the initial prototype for our project is below.

        In this prototype there is only one screen, the project page. To navigate from mobile to web, the user must click the logo button on the top left. In this, clicking the plus and minus buttons will add to the current stitch as well as update the rows when necessary. The progress indicator will update every row. This works both incrementing and deincrementing. Clicking on the number between the two buttons, the incrementor, it will increase that value by one per click. This number will reset to zero either at eight or thirteen. Clicking on plus or minus with this new number will work as expected.

User Testing

To ensure that our project truely addressed the problem we were trying to solve as well

as test for problems and opportunities, we performed two rounds of user testing. In general, the testers were college aged with either no knowledge or a beginning understanding of knitting or crocheting. For each test we had initial script as well as asked two screening questions: familiarity with technology as well as knowledge about crocheting or knitting. After that we showed the home/initial page and asked what they believe they could do on the site as well as their initial thoughts. After we gave the story: increase one row on the project Chubby Dino, and then watched to see how they accomplished it. Throughout we encouraged them to talk and at the end we asked for any thoughts or criticisms that they had through the process.

        Through user testing we determined a consistent theme of our problems. The first is, how disconnected the whole project felt from reality. In the initial prototype there was only one page and the lack of navigation or other features made it difficult to feel engaged in the product as a whole. To fix this, we ended up adding a home page as well as a menu to help with navigating around the app and adding more context to what project they were working on as a whole. We also found that a lot of our input sections were glossed over leading people to just click around until they figure out what was useful or not. To help this, we added some signifiers via text as well as a drop down icon to show that items were modifiable. This leads into another we had of a reliance on clicking, which at this stage we added a dropdown to minimize the amount of clicks needed per row.

        Other changes we made were moving some text closer and further apart due to some confusion on what it related to. The biggest example is the current stitch section where the label being centrally located made it confusing which one it applied to. And finally, for mobile, we added access to other pages to mirror the desktop application as well as add context for what the user will be able to click and use.

Colleague Assessment


        After these adjustments, we then went to the colleague assessment which essentially served as additional user tests for our project. The most important we learned from them: our changes to reduce clicking, didn’t remove the problem. While our prototype was responsive, it lacked clarity on what was a mobile feature and what was web version only. It also pointed out some problems we had seen earlier but overlooked for larger immediate changes such as icons. While we had icons, they were confusing throughout the whole process and going through this made us realize that it was excess noise.

It also made us realize that we were designing for a certain type of knitter and crocheter that has a one piece project with a traditional pattern form. It didn’t take into consideration crochet graphs or colorwork patterns nor other less popular versions of the hobby like Tunisian crochet. This is something we had to accept in the mean time. We do have a section for references that this type of pattern and graphs could go into, but it was not specifically designed for all types of crochet or knitting.

If we were to go through the process again, I would want to clarify whether or not I was testing for mobile or web version. I ended up doing both, which to a lack of clarity in the colleague assessment as a whole. As for the process itself, it would have been nice to be with someone closer to our target demographic to see if it was matching that expectations.

Final Prototype

        Taking into consideration all of the feedback as well as figuring out what was achievable for us, we ended up with the prototype below.

        It follows the same structure as the first prototype, but now has a home screen to navigate from project to project. Taking in some of the feedback we changed the colors as well as added a better way to navigate from mobile to web view via clicking the home menu and then clicking the corresponding view. By clicking on a project it takes the user to that project’s page where they can then see their pattern and increment their stitches accordingly.

From the initial, we have improved the general flow of the prototype as well as cleaned up some of the initial flaws. One of the biggest flaws throughout the testing process was the reliance the app had on clicking. We attempted to mitigate this by adding in a way to increment the amount added per stitch. This ended up help treat the symptoms of the problem, but not the cause. In this final rendition we added a increment by rows option as well as a way to manually override progress. The addition of both of them should help

        Another thing we fixed was the inconsistencies from mobile to web version. Although not pictured within this draft, we had a second version of the prototype in between which added some features and text. Since we worked on the sections individually and at separate times, the functionality of buttons as well as text labels were not the same across both platforms. We went through and checked all of them now to make sure users get the same experience, regardless of if they are in the app or on the web.

Design Values

        Throughout the project, we have been able to be true to our main two goals: creativity and accessibility. We were able to incorporate themes of creativity via the colors as well as allowing users to upload their own images and references to customize the project view how they want. The reference section in particular can be useful for people wanting to freehand or create moodboards of what they want and begin designing from there. For accessibility, in the sense of ease of use, we were able to make all of the pages easy to navigate around as well as make easy to reach and click buttons. Each section is clearly labeled and there is minimal moving once a project has been selected, keeping focus on the work and not using our application.
